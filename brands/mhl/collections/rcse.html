UK Medical Heritage Library

Search and view the collection of over 66,000 digitised European medical publications from the nineteenth century

Royal College of Surgeons of England

The Royal College of Surgeons of England (RCS) is an independent professional body committed to promoting and advancing the highest standards of surgical care for patients. Its origins lie in the Company of Barber-Surgeons, established by Act of Parliament in 1540.

Serious collecting of books at RCS only began in 1807, initially to support the work of curators of the Hunterian Museum. By the close of the 19th century, however, it was widely recognised that the RCS possessed one of the finest libraries for the medical sciences in Europe. Today the library holds approximately 75,000 books from the fifteenth century onwards, 30,640 pamphlets dating from the sixteenth century, and around 2,000 runs of periodicals, including early journals dating back to the seventeenth century.

A key focus for the UK Medical Heritage Library digitisation are examples of the tracts and pamphlets, a discrete collection that comprises 30,640 items dating from the sixteenth century up to the early twentieth century. Each item is well catalogued online thanks to grants from the Wellcome Trust's Research Resources programme. Around 21,800 items were digitised for this project, reflecting the breadth and depth of the collection's coverage. Grounds for exclusion included size, physical condition or age.

Subject scope is very wide, covering all branches of medicine and some of the natural sciences, in particular natural history, anthropology and palaeontology, which were the interests of the contemporary curators of the College museum. There is material to support research into the beginnings of anaesthesia, antisepsis, microbiology, forensic medicine, demography and epidemiology; the reform of the medical and dental professions; military medicine; and, of course, the development of specific surgical techniques. Moreover, pamphlets on sanitation and sewerage, on workhouse hospitals and on the Contagious Diseases Acts illustrate the wider history of public health and social reform.

Access to the originals:
The library collections are available via Copac (www.copac.ac.uk) and the RCS collections catalogue, Surgicat+: https://scp.rcseng.ac.uk. The library and archive collections are currently the subject of a project to complete online cataloguing by the end of 2016. Researchers are advised to contact the library to confirm holdings and availability, particularly for the early modern books and historical periodicals.
