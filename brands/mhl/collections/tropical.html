London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine

The London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine is one of the world's leading centres for research and postgraduate education in public and global health.

Founded in 1899, as the London School of Tropical Medicine, by Sir Patrick Manson, it is believed that a Library service was available to students from the beginning. However, the majority of the volumes which make up its historical collection were purchased when the School expanded to become the London School of Hygiene & Tropical Medicine. This was made possible via a grant from the Carnegie United Kingdom Trust, awarded in 1927. This collection has been supplemented by donations from a variety of prominent friends of the School, including Andrew Balfour, Shirley Murphy and Arthur Newsholme. A second collection of items on smallpox and vaccination, collected by Dr Richard Reece, a medical inspector with the Local Government Board, was sold to the School on his death. Reece's collection was described by the British Medical Journal as 'the most complete library in this country of the old literature of small-pox, variolation and vaccination.'

Together, the collections outline the history of tropical medicine and public health. They contain many volumes from the Victorian age of exploration and sanitary improvements, with particular strengths in smallpox, vaccination, cholera, malaria, and public health, and contain a fine collection of pamphlet material, as well as larger texts. It was decided that all items meeting the Medical Heritage Library criteria would be selected for digitisation.

Access to the originals:
Original materials are available to access from the reading room in London, depending on availability owing to offsite storage of some items. General information, contact details and access to the online catalogue can be found here.
